# Memory: Calvin Finch

## Personal Profile

Calvin Elisabet Finch is a third-generation Minnesotan whose paternal family settled in the Twin Cities area. Her great-grandfather emigrated from Sweden in 1908 and built furniture in St. Paul; the cabinets in her parents' house are his. She holds a Doctor of Physical Therapy from Northland University (2004) and a B.S. in Kinesiology from the same institution (2001).

She moves through the world like someone who has watched too many bodies betray their owners to believe in the illusion of permanence. There is a stillness to her that people find either reassuring or unsettling, depending on whether they have something they would rather not be seen. She does not perform warmth; she demonstrates it through the quality of her attention.

She is decisive without being impulsive, a distinction it took her most of her thirties to master. Two decades of clinical practice taught her that the first intervention is rarely the right one, so she pauses before recommending anything. The pause is calibration, not hesitation. She is allergic to performance, does not curate her language for social acceptability, and has gained a small number of friendships that go decades deep as a result.

The Scandinavian-Minnesotan inheritance shows up in small ways: the reflex toward understatement, bringing food rather than words when someone is grieving, a deep tolerance for cold, butter on almost everything, and the Lutheran childhood she has not formally practiced in twenty years but which still shapes what a Sunday should feel like. Her grandmother believed fresh air cured everything, and Calvin has caught herself sending the kids outside with the same conviction.

She believes the body keeps a record of everything the mind tries to forget. She believes in maintenance over rescue, consistency over intensity, and walking beside people long enough that they figure out their own way out. She is careful what she makes a personality trait, because the things you announce become the things you have to keep proving. She does not say she is a runner; she runs.

## Work & Projects

Calvin is a Senior Physical Therapist at Lakeview Rehabilitation Associates, a private outpatient orthopedic clinic in Minneapolis with six PTs on staff. She joined in 2014 under Dr. Priya Anand and considers Priya both her boss and a long-term professional anchor. Hours are Monday to Friday, 8:00 AM to 4:30 PM CT, with a lunch block from 12:00 to 12:45 PM. She typically sees ten to twelve patients per day.

Specializations: post-surgical rehabilitation for knee, shoulder, and hip; running-injury prevention; and geriatric mobility. Her practice is evidence-based and patient-centered, with detailed home exercise programs and an expectation that patients do their work between visits.

She attends two to three professional development workshops per year. Continuing education runs through the clinic's continuing-education portal, with certificates filed in cloud storage. She contributes one short patient-education column per quarter to the clinic's newsletter.

## Interests & Hobbies

Running is daily verification that she still exists in a body she trusts. She has been running since she was sixteen, when her cross-country coach noticed an unusually efficient stride. She tracks training in a paper notebook beside the Garmin app because she does not trust software with anything she cannot recover by hand. She rereads her notes in February for patterns.

Sourdough is the other thing. The starter is named Margot by Astrid in a moment of teenage whimsy Calvin secretly loved. She bakes two loaves on alternating Saturdays. The process is the appeal: the dough's resistance, the way the crust sounds when you tap it. There is a version of her that would happily quit physical therapy to run a tiny bakery. She has decided, repeatedly, not to listen.

She reads literary fiction at a pace of about one book every three weeks, slower in racing season. She reads running memoirs less for the running and more for what people make meaning of when they are exhausted and alone with their thoughts.

- **Communication style**: direct, concise, no fluff. Information organized and actionable. Do not over-explain.
- **Notification preferences**: clinic-related items during work hours only; family items anytime; minimize non-essential pings.
- **Shopping**: grocery delivery on Saturdays via Northgate Foods when possible. Running gear from specialty stores after in-person fittings; two pairs of shoes at a time, rotated, replaced at 400 miles. Household items from Target or online. Researches before any real purchase; usually waits a week. Bought used furniture for most of the house and is mildly proud of this.
- **Gifts**: remembers what people mentioned wanting and gives it three months later. Generous, never flashy. Charitable giving is quiet.
- **Entertainment**: podcasts on running, science, and true crime. Literary fiction and running memoirs. One TV series at a time with Erik, currently a crime drama.
- **Music**: indie folk and acoustic playlists for cooking; upbeat electronic for running.
- **Aesthetic**: natural fibers, muted colors, nothing that announces itself. Weekday uniform of dark slim pants and a fitted long-sleeve top in cream, olive, or navy with leather sneakers. Weekend jeans and a sweater she has owned for nine years. Does not enjoy shopping for clothes.
- **Food and drink**: salmon with dill, roasted root vegetables, grandmother's meatballs (penciled on an index card in a tin box), sourdough bread, hearty winter soups. Coffee is black. Occasional weekend wine. Cooks most meals at home.
- **Travel**: small inns over hotels, walkable towns over resorts, empty beaches over swim-up bars. Packs carry-on only. Brings one book, a notebook, and running shoes. Would rather repeat a trip she loved than try something new. Ely lake cabin nine times. Dream trip: a slow walking tour of the Scottish Highlands in autumn, unbooked because the kids are still small.
- **Comfort and decompression**: 28-minute neighborhood walking loop with Hazel watching from the front window. Baking when anxious; Erik knows not to ask until she is about thirty minutes in. Red wine on Fridays, second coffee on Saturday mornings, the front porch in summer or the wood stove in winter, often not talking. When she is really sad, she goes to her grandmother's meatball recipe and cooks for the house.
- **Social style**: not introverted in the textbook sense, but not entertained by small talk that goes nowhere. Prefers two or three people over ten, dinner over a party, a long walk over a brunch. Gravitates toward kitchens and dogs at parties. Excellent at exits.
- **Sensory loves**: pine, woodsmoke, baking bread, a clean dog, her father's old workshop. Damp leaves in October. The first sharp cold morning of winter. Erik's shampoo. Footsteps on hardwood. A kettle whistling. The dense quiet of snowfall. Heavy ceramic mugs. Clean sheets. Cold water on her face after a hard run.
